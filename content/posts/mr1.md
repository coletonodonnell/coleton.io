The Donor is a very unique song. I actually don't know too many songs like it, and it stands alone in both quality and content. The track is simply put hypnotic. By the end of the arrangement, its purpose is made clear by the blending of religious elements throughout the track. For instance, she uses a play on words to switch the often used “lead us not into temptation" to “leave us not forsaken," quoting Psalms 27:9. This leaves the listener first reminded of the "lead us not into temptation" and then reflecting on the "leave us not forsaken." This gives off the impression sonically of a demand towards God to stop leading creation into sin but then moving the listener to a different demand: a demand towards God to not abandon Sill, to not abandon you, and to not abandon the world at large. Indeed, Sill intended the track to be a sort of message and plea towards God. In her words:

> Most of my songs, I always try to write them so they’ll make people feel better, or make them feel that their warm, human spirit is affirmed… but I thought one day when I was depressed, you know when you’re real depressed and you see everything comes to nothing, well, I thought, maybe I ought to take a different approach, and write a song that, instead of directed at people, would somehow musically induce God into giving us all a break, cause I was getting a little fed up by this point. So I put some combinations of notes in there that I worked on a long time hoping it would work… since that time I’ve decided that I shouldn’t get any more breaks, cause I already squandered them in weird places. But I’d like to sing this song for you in the hope that you’ll get a break.

Interestingly, the most ethereal and unique section of the arrangement are the choral segments. These were originally written with 6 voices in mind, but soon after finishing composition it was actually moved to a whopping 40 voices! The powerful choral parts are taken from a very old traditional Greek chant called the Κύριε, or Kyrie in English. It is quite amazing how the arrangement was built around the actual chant and how it weaves beautifully in and out of the chant. 

Tragically Judee Sill died at the age of 35 due to an overdose. Long battles with drug usage led to a very deep depression for Sill, and this created a constant deep dark loop of substance abuse. Even within the commentary for *The Donor*, she believed she "shouldn’t get any more breaks." One has to wonder what other masterpieces she could've produced if her time was not cut short.
