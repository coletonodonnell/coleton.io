> The farther one gets into the wilderness, the greater is the attraction of its lonely freedom.
~ President Theodore Roosevelt

President Roosevelt is my favorite President. Not because of his policy, but of his charisma, character, and his ambition for preservation. He is definitely someone I wished I could have sat down and talked to. President Roosevelt is famous for the thing he loved most - the natural beauty of the United States, particularly the American West. The National Park system is his creation, and the preservation of such natural wonders likewise. I believe that President Roosevelt understood the effect that industrialization has on the human psyche more than anyone else of his time, at the very least understood it broadest. Human necessity is often boiled down to the simplest terms: food, water, shelter, and clothing. Though these nourish the body, what nourishes the mind and soul? The answer is nature, desire, art, and affection. These are just as necessary as the physical, these are the core elements of love.

If President Roosevelt were alive to listen to Robbie Basho’s American Primitive Masterpiece *Visions of the Country*, I believe it would have been a Top 10 album for him, if not Top 5. This is because Visions of the Country captures the essence and beauty of the Nature of the American West like none other. In the liner notes to the Album, Basho writes a passage about the meaning of the album as a description of times past, before the effects of industrialization. The first sentence states:

> I would paint for you a portrait of North America, as a beautiful woman, when she was young and untamed, untrammeled upon and unshamed.

Every song on this album is a masterpiece through and through, and when malling over what song to even focus on I struggled. "Orphan’s Lament" is a touching song, possibly Basho’s most introspective. Basho was orphaned as a child, and in this song, Basho begs his parents to return to him and to love him again. "Rocky Mountain Raga" is by far the most peaceful song, acting in the words of Basho as a “musical statement portraying the Love Tryst between Man, Nature, and the Almighty.” These two tracks are highlights for me and I would feel horrible not mentioning them, but my absolute most favorite song off the album is "Night Way." The song is an ode to the Southwest and is inspired by the Navajo Nightway chant. It slowly builds up to this sort of chant, and then at the climax, Basho begins scat singing, blending his voice as an instrument with his guitar. Slowly the chant dies off and we’re left with the initial instrumental. I don’t think that I have heard a song capture the feeling of being in nature as much as this song. The way Basho pours his heart into the song is truly majestic. Basho is almost channeling the Navajo chant through him rather than actually singing it. Basho truly becomes a part of the music itself. I can’t really comment more on the song except listen to it and experience its affect first hand.
