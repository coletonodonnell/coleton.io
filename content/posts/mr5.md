What I love about music is its power to bridge cultures and peoples because it represents emotions. Arguably the strongest human emotion is love, and as a result love is a focal point of a lot of music. The greatest album I know of regarding romance, love, and desire has to be *The Creek Drank the Cradle* by Iron & Wine. The folk album considers themes of loss, growing up, parents, your significant other, and where you grew up. No matter the subject matter, the theme revolves around the love of said thing. My favourite song in the album is the first track, "Lion's Mane," a beautiful piece that describes with full honesty what it is like to be in love, all the while painted under a beautiful instrumental. Love is not an ideal or perfect process, it is a wonderful yet chaotic consequence of dependency and an outlet for emotional baggage. The song is split into four verses which I will dissect and analyse: 

> Run like a race for family  
When you hear like you're alone  
The rusted gears of morning  
To faceless busy phones  
We gladly run in circles  
But the shape we meant to make is gone  

I interpret the first verse as an argument in favour of why we love, to escape the monotony of life. The "faceless busy phones" are the jobs all must partake in and the "circles" are the cycles of repetition day in and day out until "the shape we meant to make is gone," eg. why do we work towards anything anyway? The answer is love. We work to spend time with family and enjoy company, to further our love. But, how far can we get stuck in our own circle before we forget the entire goal of entering that circle? Love is what we work towards, anything that limits that love is a waste of time.

> Love is a tired symphony  
Tou hum when you're awake  
Love is a crying baby  
Mama warned you not to shake  
Love is the best sensation  
Hiding in the lion's mane  

After the introduction of sorts, love is defined with metaphors to capture its excellence. Love is a "tired symphony," a well worn and classic tune that you can rely on. Love is a "crying baby Mama warned you not to shake," meaning that love is bothersome at times, but it is best not to "shake" or disrupt its process. Finally, love is "the best sensation hiding in the lion's mane." A lion's mane can be described as silky and smooth, enjoyable to run your fingers through and soft to the touch. It is something that is pleasant, but inversely incredibly dangerous: you are right next to the lion's jaw. "Lion's Mane" paints a dilemma: love is extreme and its outcome either joyous or dangerous.

> So I'll clear the road, the gravel  
And the thornbush in your path  
That burns a scented oil  
That I'll drip into your bath  
The water's there to warm you  
And the earth is warmer  
When you laugh  

The 3rd verse is easily the hardest to interpret, but I view it as the benefits and drawbacks to love. The road in question is the endeavour for love, and the "gravel" and "thornbush" are the hardships and hurdles to overcome in a relationship. Yet, those hurdles, the "thornbush" burns the "scented oil." In this way, if you are able to overcome the hurdles of love, you are given something far more beautiful. To bathe someone is a very intimate act, not something that you do with someone randomly, so this description acts as the outcome. My favourite line comes next: "The water's there to warm you; And the earth is warmer When you laugh." The water is not just a reflection of intimacy, but also acts as a metaphor for the love you can have for someone. The water warms you, and "the earth," or you, is warmer when the one you love is happy. This verse states that love is a mutual embrace, but it requires effort to get there.

> Love is a scene I render  
When you catch me wide awake  
Love's a dream you enter  
Though I shake and shake and shake you  
Love is the best endeavor  
Waiting in the lion's mane  

The 4th and final verse echoes the 2nd, building off the themes presented there. It wraps up with the a very similar conclusion to that of the 2nd, basically switching "sensation" to "endeavour" and "hiding" to "waiting." This is a fitting end to the song, as it summarizes it completely. Love is beautiful but dangerous, you will get hurt as you are exposing your vulnerability to someone, and it isn't guaranteed that person is going to stick around or that person is going to love you for who you are. Yet, the "endeavour" for love is the "best" thing you can do. In the end, its message is clear: embrace the lion's mane, you won't be sorry.
